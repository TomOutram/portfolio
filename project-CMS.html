Designing for a CMS

Overview

The Ministry of Education recently updated its main website, education.govt.nz. The goal was to create a modern, user-friendly site that aligned with the ministry's branding.

The new site was built on Drupal CMS (content management system), allowing different ministry groups to load and manage their own content.

As the designer, I focused on creating UI components that worked well with the CMS, enabling users to add content using pre-designed templates while maintaining a consistent look, clear text, and easy-to-navigate information.

Challenges

User loaded content - When designing for a CMS, I had to consider how users would populate the content. The components needed to be flexible and well thought out, taking into account title length, character limits, and different content types or states.

Changing requirements - The project had many changing requirements and redesigns. It was important to adapt quickly as things evolved. Our team had to track versions, make fast adjustments, and stay on schedule to deliver on time.

Different users - We had to design for a wide range of users, including teachers, admin staff, parents, students, suppliers, and more. We also needed to consider internal ministry staff who would access the site and manage its content.

Design iterations

another appropriate component pic - more iterations perhaps

The site's components went through several revisions before we settled on designs that worked for CMS content loading, met accessibility standards, and responded effectively on smaller screen sizes.

The blocks were designed for reuse and intended to be integrated into our design system for future projects. They followed the design system's guidelines for colours, fonts, spacing, and other specifications.

Landing page components

homepage news and carousel design
homepage news and carousel in action

The site has a main homepage and separate landing pages for different audiences. When content is loaded in the CMS, it's tagged for a specific audience and automatically displayed on the appropriate landing page.This ensures users only see relevant content that’s targeted at them. I designed cards and carousel components to showcase this targeted audience content.

Article page components

article type page with block examples
CMS example that shows off the options for all of the different blocks

The most common page templates on the site were article pages, similar to those on news or blog sites. We provided various block elements for users to include via the CMS such as; text blocks, collapsible content, images, multimedia, signposts, contact info, and a lot more. Each block was designed to fit seamlessly into articles, offering flexibility and utility for creating a range of content to meet users' needs.

Component properties

variations of component - like alert block
CMS options and figma settings for block

The attributes and adjustable settings for each component were designed to be intuitive, so users could easily adjust customise pages. Our goal was to offer powerful tools without overcomplicating the process, letting users present content in the way they wanted.

These attributes and settings were also reflected in our Figma design library using component properties, allowing us to quickly build and prototype pages. This helped us test and validate designs before development.

Handoff and specifications

dev mode specs on one or two components
dev mode more specs
page layout with dev mode specs hidden and my notes stuff (reworked)

I worked closely with web developers throughout the design process, using Atlassian Jira to create and manage tasks. I provided user stories, wireframes, and design specs, which developers then implemented. We communicated through comments, tracked progress, and often met in person to resolve issues and collaborate.

I used Figma’s ‘dev mode’ to share component specs, including colours, spacing, fonts, and other details from our design system. This gave developers clear, accurate specs, reducing the need for revisions, often resulting in pixel-perfect results first time. Any special notes or annotations were added in Figma with comments tagged for developers.

By working so closely with the devs, and using such detailed and comprehensive specs, the design handoff became a very streamlined and enjoyable process.

Component library

By the end of the project, we had a solid library of components and templates for content loaders to utilise across the various parts of the site. These components also bolstered our design system, able to be reused in future projects.

Specs and notes were recorded in our Figma component library and knowledge articles within Atlassian Confluence.

Summary

Outcomes - We didn’t get everything right at first. As teams started uploading content, we realised many adjustments were needed, and this will likely continue throughout the product’s lifecycle. It’s tough to predict the needs and behaviour of such a diverse group of users and content. Thankfully, our strong, efficient relationship with the developers made it easy to handle these changes.

What did I learn? - This project taught me a lot about designing a large, content-heavy website on a CMS.

  • I had to make key design decisions to ensure it worked for different types of content and was adaptable for the future.
  • I had to balance the needs of content managers and end users. This challenged me to be flexible and agile in my design approach.
  • I worked closely with web developers, building a strong and cohesive partnership. I gained valuable experience with design handoff and creating clear design specs.

Next steps - The site will keep evolving based on user feedback. As we add more content and sections, new components may be needed. This gives our team the chance to refine our skills and create a site that makes content loading easier for internal groups and offers a better experience for users.
